When playing Filipino Mahjong, Wind and Dragon tiles are also considered flowers.

An Pair is a set of any two of the same tile, a Pong is a set of any three of the same tile, and a Kong is the set of all four copies of the same tile.

A Chow is a run of any three numbered tiles (i.e. Dots, Bamboos, or Characters).

In hand

— Meld —>

After you steal, you must meld (i.e. “lock in”) the Pong, Kong, or Chow you made by revealing these tiles to the table face-up.

Tiles in a meld can no longer be discarded, and must be part of your final Mahjong.

Finally, you must discard a tile to offset the one you stole. Play continues as if you had just finished your turn, skipping anyone in between you and whoever discarded.

Remember that you cannot steal to complete a Pair.

Special Rules for Melding Kongs

Even though a Kong is equivalent to a Pong or a Chow in terms of getting a Mahjong, it takes one more tile to form. Thus, for every Kong you want to use in a Mahjong, you will need an extra tile. This is the justification for these three rules:

1. Whenever you meld a Kong, you must immediately draw a tile.

Kong in hand

— Concealed Meld —>

2. If it is your turn and you have a Kong in your hand that you have decided you want to use as part of your final Mahjong, you must meld it by calling “Kong!” and placing the tiles face-down. This is called a Concealed Kong. Remember to draw.

Drawn

+

— Add to Meld —>

3. If it is your turn and you draw the fourth tile of one of your melded Pongs, you may add to your meld and turn it into a melded Kong. Remember to draw.

A rarely encountered edge-case to this final rule is that if another player adds to a melded Pong to form a melded Kong, but you can use that tile to complete a Mahjong, then you can rob their Kong (i.e. take the fourth tile) and win.

Remember that you cannot steal to add to a melded Pong to form a melded Kong.

Example Scoring System

When the game ends, either by someone getting Mahjong or there being no more tiles to draw, everyone's score is calculated and money/chips/points are distributed.

The way the score is calculated varies tremendously between versions of Mahjong. This is one example of a scoring system, based on Filipino Mahjong:

When Score Name Description
Win 4 Winning Completed a Mahjong
2 Seven Pairs Completed a Seven Pairs Mahjong
1 All Pongs Mahjong with only Pongs/Kongs, no Chows
All Chows Mahjong with only Chows, no Pongs/Kongs
Concealed Mahjong with no melds, except concealed melds
Revealed Mahjong where every Pong/Kong/Chow was melded before winning – no concealed melds
Win or Lose 2 Straight Hand contains 1-9 of a single suit in Chows
Flush Hand contains only tiles from one suit
Concealed Kong Made a Concealed Kong
Pong to Kong Made a melded Kong from a melded Pong
1 Stolen Kong Made a melded Kong from a stolen tile
Lose 1 Difficult Wait Needed one specific tile to win
Back to Back Hand contains two Pairs, needed one tile to win

When playing Filipino Mahjong, or any version where Winds and Dragons are considered flowers, these are the rules for awarding bonus points:

Score Name Description
2 No Flower Mahjong Complete a Mahjong with no flowers
1 No Flower Loss Lose with no flowers
No Flower Deal Dealt no flowers at the start of the game
Thirteen Flowers Collect thirteen flowers

Otherwise, you get 1 point for 2 flowers, 2 for 4 flowers, and 3 for all 8 flowers.

Sources and Caveats

This reference sheet is based on the general rules of Mahjong as described by the Mahjong Picture Guide and Wikipedia. The scoring system is based on Filipino Mahjong as described by the Mahjong Wiki, Mahjongero, Theresa A, and Jason Singleton.

The rule changes for 16-tile Mahjong are based on Taiwanese Mahjong, as described by Mahjong Time, and Filipino Mahjong, as described by the sources above.

All tile graphics are based on the Wikimedia illustrations created by Cangjie6.

Caveats: Not included are the way tiles are set up and distributed at the beginning of the game, the way different rounds of the game are coordinated, and the additional rare winning hands added by many versions of Mahjong.
